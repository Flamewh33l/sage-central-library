# Herman Melville

## Overview
Herman Melville (1819–1891) was an American novelist, short story writer, and poet. Best known for "Moby-Dick," he was largely forgotten during his lifetime but is now recognized as one of the greatest American writers. His work explores obsession, the nature of evil, the limits of knowledge, and humanity's place in an indifferent universe.

## Major Works

### "Moby-Dick; or, The Whale" (1851)
- His masterpiece, ignored during his lifetime
- Captain Ahab's obsessive hunt for the white whale
- Part novel, part philosophy, part encyclopedia of whaling
- Explores obsession, revenge, fate, and the nature of evil

### "Billy Budd, Sailor" (published posthumously, 1924)
- Tale of innocence destroyed by institutional injustice
- Billy Budd — the handsome sailor
- Captain Vere — the conflicted authority
- Good vs. evil, law vs. morality

### "Bartleby, the Scrivener" (1853)
- "I would prefer not to"
- Passive resistance and existential despair
- The walls of Wall Street
- One of the first great office stories

### "Typee" (1846)
- Based on his experience in the Marquesas Islands
- Romanticized view of "savage" life
- Critique of Western civilization
- His first popular success

### "The Confidence-Man" (1857)
- His last novel, satire of American optimism
- Characters on a steamboat, all potentially con artists
- Questions authenticity and trust

## Key Themes

### Obsession and Monomania
- Ahab's single-minded pursuit of the whale
- Obsession consumes everything else
- "All my means are sane, my motive and my object mad."

### The Unknowable
- The white whale resists interpretation
- Is it evil? God? Nature? Nothing?
- Some truths cannot be known
- "All visible objects, man, are but as pasteboard masks."

### Fate vs. Free Will
- Is Ahab fated to chase the whale?
- Prophecies and omens throughout Moby-Dick
- The line between determination and choice

### Good and Evil
- Not simple categories
- The whale: evil or just nature?
- Ahab: villain or tragic hero?
- Billy Budd: innocent but kills; Claggart: evil but lawful

### The Sublime and Terror
- Nature as beautiful and terrifying
- The whale represents the sublime — overwhelming, uncontrollable
- Human smallness in cosmic perspective

### Isolation and Community
- Ishmael survives through connection (Queequeg)
- Ahab's isolation destroys him
- "I try all things; I achieve what I can."

## Key Characters

### Captain Ahab (Moby-Dick)
- Obsessed, charismatic, terrifying
- Lost his leg to the white whale
- Will sacrifice everything — ship, crew, self — for revenge
- "I'd strike the sun if it insulted me."

### Ishmael (Moby-Dick)
- The narrator, the survivor
- Open-minded, curious, seeking connection
- Represents tolerance and friendship (with Queequeg)
- "Call me Ishmael."

### Queequeg (Moby-Dick)
- South Sea Islander harpooner
- Ishmael's friend despite cultural differences
- Noble savage trope, but also genuine humanity
- Dies saving others

### Bartleby (Bartleby, the Scrivener)
- The passive resister
- "I would prefer not to"
- Existential refusal to engage
- Dies alone, staring at a wall

### Billy Budd (Billy Budd)
- The innocent, the beautiful
- Stutters when emotional
- Kills Claggart in a moment of passion
- Executed for a crime he didn't mean to commit

## The White Whale as Symbol

The whale resists single interpretation:
- Nature's indifference to humanity
- God (inscrutable, terrible)
- Evil (what took Ahab's leg)
- The unconscious (cannot be seen directly)
- The unattainable (always out of reach)
- Truth itself (some things cannot be known)

## Impact on Cognitive Development

1. **Tolerance for ambiguity** — Not everything can be resolved
2. **Understanding obsession** — How fixation destroys
3. **The limits of knowledge** — Some truths remain hidden
4. **Nature's indifference** — The universe doesn't care about us
5. **The cost of revenge** — Ahab's destruction
6. **Cultural openness** — Ishmael and Queequeg's friendship
7. **Institutional critique** — Systems vs. individuals (Billy Budd, Bartleby)

## Memorable Quotes

- "Call me Ishmael."
- "It is not down on any map; true places never are."
- "I know not all that may be coming, but be it what it will, I'll go to it laughing."
- "There is a wisdom that is woe; but there is a woe that is madness."
- "To the last, I grapple with thee; from hell's heart, I stab at thee; for hate's sake, I spit my last breath at thee."
- "He who has never failed somewhere, that man can not be great."

## Historical Context
- Whaling era America
- Pre-Civil War tensions
- Expansionism and manifest destiny
- Melville's own whaling experiences
- Friendship with Nathaniel Hawthorne

## Criticisms to Consider
- Moby-Dick is notoriously difficult
- Dense, digressive, encyclopedic
- Some racial language of the era
- Can feel meandering to modern readers

## Influence
- Rediscovered in 1920s
- Influenced modernism
- Environmental themes prescient
- Psychological depth before psychology

## Connection to Other Authors
- Contemporary of Hawthorne (influence)
- Psychological depth like Dostoevsky
- Existential themes presage existentialism
- Nature themes complement Transcendentalists
