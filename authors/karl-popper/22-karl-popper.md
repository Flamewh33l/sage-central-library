# Karl Popper

## Overview
Karl Popper (1902–1994) was an Austrian-British philosopher, academic, and social commentator. He's considered one of the greatest philosophers of science of the 20th century. His work on falsification revolutionized how we understand scientific knowledge, and his political philosophy defended liberal democracy against totalitarianism.

## Major Works

### "The Logic of Scientific Discovery" (1934)
- Falsification as criterion for science
- Critique of induction
- Foundation of philosophy of science

### "The Open Society and Its Enemies" (1945)
- Defense of liberal democracy
- Critique of Plato, Hegel, Marx
- Written during WWII as response to totalitarianism

### "Conjectures and Refutations" (1963)
- Essays on knowledge and science
- "All life is problem solving"
- Accessible introduction to his thought

### "The Poverty of Historicism" (1957)
- Critique of historical determinism
- Against the idea that history follows laws
- We cannot predict the future of society

## Key Concepts

### Falsifiability
- Science must make predictions that could be proven wrong
- If a theory can't be falsified, it's not scientific
- "A theory that is not refutable by any conceivable event is non-scientific."
- The demarcation problem: science vs. non-science

### The Problem of Induction
- We can't prove general rules from specific observations
- No matter how many white swans you see, you haven't proven all swans are white
- Science doesn't prove theories true — it fails to prove them false
- "No number of observations of white swans can prove that all swans are white. A single observation of a black swan can prove that not all swans are white."

### Conjecture and Refutation
- Science proceeds by bold guesses (conjectures)
- Then attempts to refute them through testing
- Surviving theories are not proven, just not yet falsified
- "Science must begin with myths, and with the criticism of myths."

### The Open Society
- Society that allows criticism and change
- No final authority, no unquestioned truth
- "The open society is one in which men have learned to be critical of taboos, and to base decisions on the authority of their own intelligence."
- Contrast with tribal/closed societies

### Piecemeal Social Engineering
- Make small, reversible changes
- Avoid grand utopian plans
- "The piecemeal engineer will adopt the method of searching for, and fighting against, the greatest and most urgent evils of society, rather than searching for, and fighting for, its greatest ultimate good."
- Humility in social design

### Critique of Historicism
- History doesn't follow inevitable laws
- We can't predict the future course of society
- "The belief that history is determined by laws is a superstition."
- Against Marx's historical determinism

### The Paradox of Tolerance
- "Unlimited tolerance must lead to the disappearance of tolerance."
- If we tolerate the intolerant, they will destroy tolerance
- "We should therefore claim, in the name of tolerance, the right not to tolerate the intolerant."
- A society must be willing to defend itself

### Three World Theory
- **World 1**: Physical world
- **World 2**: Mental states, subjective experience
- **World 3**: Products of the human mind (ideas, theories, art)
- World 3 interacts with World 1 through World 2

## Impact on Cognitive Development

1. **Scientific thinking** — Looking for disconfirmation, not confirmation
2. **Intellectual humility** — Our best theories might be wrong
3. **Critical attitude** — Questioning authority and dogma
4. **Fallibilism** — All knowledge is provisional
5. **Tolerance paradox** — Some limits on tolerance preserve tolerance
6. **Piecemeal improvement** — Small changes over grand plans
7. **Anti-determinism** — Future is open, not fixed

## Memorable Quotes

- "Science must begin with myths, and with the criticism of myths."
- "Our knowledge can only be finite, while our ignorance must necessarily be infinite."
- "True ignorance is not the absence of knowledge, but the refusal to acquire it."
- "No rational argument will have a rational effect on a man who does not want to adopt a rational attitude."
- "The more we learn about the world, and the deeper our learning, the more conscious, specific, and articulate will be our knowledge of what we do not know."
- "We are not students of some subject matter, but students of problems."

## Historical Context
- Born in Vienna, Jewish background (converted)
- Witnessed rise of Nazism, fled Austria
- Friend of Hayek (both at LSE)
- Debate with Wittgenstein (famous poker incident)
- Lived to see communism fall

## Criticisms

### Falsification Too Strict
- Many accepted scientific theories weren't abandoned when faced with anomalies
- Scientists sometimes ignore falsifying evidence temporarily
- Real science is messier than his model
- Kuhn's paradigm shifts offer alternative view

### Political Philosophy
- Some find his defense of liberalism too ideological
- Critics say he misread Plato, Hegel, Marx
- "Open society" can justify interventionism
- Piecemeal engineering may not address structural problems

### Problem of Induction Response
- His solution (conjecture and refutation) may not fully solve it
- Why assume the future will resemble the past?
- Some philosophers say he didn't actually solve Hume's problem

## Influence
- Philosophy of science
- Political philosophy
- Scientific method education
- Open society institutions (Soros's Open Society Foundations)

## Connection to Other Authors
- Influenced by Kant, Hume
- Friend and colleague of Hayek
- Opposite of Marx (historicism debate)
- Responded to Plato, Hegel
- Contemporary: Kuhn (paradigm shifts)

## The Popperian Attitude
Not just a set of theories but an attitude: critical, fallibilist, open to revision. "I may be wrong and you may be right, and by an effort, we may get nearer to the truth."
