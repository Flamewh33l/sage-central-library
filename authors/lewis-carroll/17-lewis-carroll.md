# Lewis Carroll

## Overview
Lewis Carroll (born Charles Lutwidge Dodgson, 1832–1898) was an English writer, mathematician, logician, and photographer. Best known for "Alice's Adventures in Wonderland" and "Through the Looking-Glass," his work blends fantasy, wordplay, logic puzzles, and surreal humor. He revolutionized children's literature and influenced countless writers, artists, and thinkers.

## Major Works

### "Alice's Adventures in Wonderland" (1865)
- Alice falls down rabbit hole
- Nonsense logic, wordplay, absurdity
- Questioning authority, identity, and meaning

### "Through the Looking-Glass, and What Alice Found There" (1871)
- Mirror world, chess game structure
- Jabberwocky poem
- "The Walrus and the Carpenter"

### "The Hunting of the Snark" (1876)
- Nonsense poem
- "An Agony in Eight Fits"
- Dark absurdism

### "Euclid and his Modern Rivals" (1879)
- Mathematical work
- Defense of Euclidean geometry
- Shows his logical rigor

### "Symbolic Logic" (1896)
- Logic textbook
- Games and puzzles
- Syllogisms and logical fallacies

## Key Themes and Techniques

### Nonsense and Logic
- Nonsense isn't random — it follows rules, just unexpected ones
- Logic pushed to absurdity reveals truth
- "Contrariwise, if it was so, it might be; and if it were so, it would be; but as it isn't, it ain't. That's logic."

### Wordplay and Language
- Portmanteau words (slithy = slimy + lithe)
- Puns, riddles, linguistic puzzles
- Questioning meaning itself
- "When I use a word, it means just what I choose it to mean."

### Identity and Transformation
- "Who in the world am I? Ah, that's the great puzzle!"
- Constant size changes
- Questioning what makes you you
- Caterpillar: "Who are YOU?"

### Authority and Absurdity
- The Queen of Hearts: "Off with her head!"
- Rules without reason
- Mocking arbitrary power
- "Begin at the beginning and go on till you come to the end: then stop."

### Dream Logic
- Events follow emotional, not physical, laws
- Transitions without explanation
- Questions without answers
- "Why is a raven like a writing desk?" (originally no answer)

### Games and Rules
- Chess structure in Looking-Glass
- Cards in Wonderland
- Rules that can be broken or questioned
- Games as metaphors for life

## Memorable Characters

### The Cheshire Cat
- "We're all mad here. I'm mad. You're mad."
- Disappears, leaving only grin
- Philosophical absurdity
- Questions what's real

### The Mad Hatter
- "Why is a raven like a writing desk?"
- Time stood still at 6:00
- "I'm not strange, weird, off, nor crazy, my reality is just different from yours."

### The Queen of Hearts
- Tyrannical absurdity
- "Off with their heads!"
- Rules without reason
- Authority without legitimacy

### The Caterpillar
- "Who are you?"
- Questions identity
- Mushroom and size changes
- Cryptic wisdom

### Humpty Dumpty
- "When I use a word, it means just what I choose it to mean."
- Linguistic control
- Fragile arrogance
- "There's glory for you!"

### The White Rabbit
- Anxiety and time obsession
- "I'm late! I'm late!"
- Initiates the adventure
- Represents adult concerns in child's world

## Impact on Cognitive Development

1. **Linguistic flexibility** — Wordplay, puns, multiple meanings
2. **Logical thinking** — Recognizing valid vs. invalid reasoning
3. **Questioning authority** — Seeing absurdity in arbitrary rules
4. **Identity exploration** — "Who am I?" as genuine question
5. **Nonsense as sense** — Finding patterns in chaos
6. **Imagination** — Dream logic, impossible worlds
7. **Critical thinking** — Questioning assumptions, language, meaning

## Historical Context
- Victorian England
- Mathematical don at Oxford
- Photography pioneer
- Controversial relationships with children (debated by scholars)
- Published under pseudonym to protect academic career

## Criticisms to Consider
- Some find the nonsense frustrating
- Victorian references can be obscure
- Darker interpretations possible
- Scholarly debate about his relationships with children
- Drug references disputed (probably not)

## Influence
- Surrealism (Dali illustrated Alice)
- Modern children's literature
- Word games, logic puzzles
- Absurdist comedy
- Countless adaptations

## Connection to Other Authors
- Influenced surrealists (Breton, etc.)
- Wordplay tradition (Joyce, Nabokov)
- Absurdist drama (Beckett, Ionesco)
- Children's literature revolution
- Logic and philosophy (Wittgenstein admired him)
