# Adam Smith

## Overview
Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish economist, philosopher, and author, often called the "father of capitalism" and "father of economics." His work laid the foundation for classical economics. Despite being claimed by free-market advocates, his actual work is more nuanced than commonly understood — he was also a moral philosopher concerned with justice and human flourishing.

## Major Works

### "The Wealth of Nations" (1776)
- Foundation of modern economics
- Division of labor, free markets, "invisible hand"
- Published same year as American Declaration of Independence

### "The Theory of Moral Sentiments" (1759)
- His first major work (often ignored)
- Sympathy, empathy, moral psychology
- The "impartial spectator" within us

### "Lectures on Jurisprudence"
- Notes from his lectures
- Legal and political theory
- Published posthumously

## Key Concepts

### The Invisible Hand
- Individuals pursuing self-interest benefit society
- "By pursuing his own interest he frequently promotes that of the society more effectually than when he really intends to promote it."
- NOT "greed is good" — context matters
- Emergent order from individual actions

### Division of Labor
- Breaking production into specialized tasks
- Dramatically increases productivity
- Pin factory example: 10 people making 48,000 pins/day
- "The greatest improvement in the productive powers of labour... seem to have been the effects of the division of labour."

### Free Trade and Markets
- Specialization requires trade
- Markets coordinate without central direction
- Comparative advantage (developed later, but consistent)
- Protectionism harms prosperity

### Self-Interest vs. Selfishness
- Self-interest is rational pursuit of goals
- Not the same as selfishness or greed
- "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest."
- We benefit each other through exchange, not charity

### The Role of Government
Smith identified THREE proper roles for government:
1. **Defense** — Protecting society from violence
2. **Justice** — Administration of justice, protection of property
3. **Public Works** — Infrastructure and education that private sector won't provide

He was NOT an anarchist or absolutist about markets.

### Sympathy and Moral Sentiments
- From "Theory of Moral Sentiments"
- Humans have natural sympathy for others
- "How selfish soever man may be supposed, there are evidently some principles in his nature, which interest him in the fortune of others."
- The impartial spectator — our internal judge

### The Impartial Spectator
- We imagine how others view our actions
- Internal moral compass
- "We endeavor to examine our own conduct as we imagine any other fair and impartial spectator would examine it."
- Foundation of conscience

### Limits of Markets
- Markets work for many things, not everything
- Education, justice, defense need government
- Monopolies are dangerous
- Business people conspire against the public: "People of the same trade seldom meet together, even for merriment and diversion, but the conversation ends in a conspiracy against the public, or in some contrivance to raise prices."

## Impact on Cognitive Development

1. **Spontaneous order** — Understanding how systems self-organize
2. **Trade as cooperation** — Exchange benefits both parties
3. **Specialization** — Division of labor creates wealth
4. **Moral psychology** — Sympathy as foundation of ethics
5. **Balanced view of self-interest** — Not greed, but rational pursuit
6. **Role of institutions** — Markets need rules, justice, infrastructure
7. **Skepticism of business** — Corporations need watching

## Memorable Quotes

- "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest."
- "No society can surely be flourishing and happy, of which the far greater part of the members are poor and miserable."
- "The real price of everything, what everything really costs to the man who wants to acquire it, is the toil and trouble of acquiring it."
- "Man is an animal that makes bargains: no other animal does this - no dog exchanges bones with another."
- "The uniform, constant, and uninterrupted effort of every man to better his condition... is frequently powerful enough to maintain the natural progress of things toward improvement."

## Historical Context
- Scottish Enlightenment
- Friend of David Hume
- Witnessed early industrial revolution
- Professor at Glasgow
- Never married, lived with mother

## What People Get Wrong About Smith

### "Greed is Good"
- No. He distinguished self-interest from greed
- He wrote extensively about sympathy and morality
- Markets work because of trust, not despite it

### "No Government"
- He explicitly identified government roles
- Defense, justice, public works
- Not a libertarian by modern standards

### "Unregulated Markets"
- He warned against monopolies
- He noted businessmen conspire against the public
- He supported some regulations

### "Capitalist Ideologue"
- He died before "capitalism" existed as concept
- He was a moral philosopher first
- More nuanced than later simplifications

## Criticisms

### Labor Theory of Value
- Later economists (Marx) developed this
- Smith's value theory incomplete
- Marginal revolution superseded it

### Distribution
- Didn't fully address inequality
- Assumed benefits would spread
- "Trickle down" not his phrase but consistent with some readings

### Colonialism
- Some passages supportive of empire
- Product of his time
- Less critical than he should have been

### Gender
- Invisible women in his economic analysis
- Unpaid labor ignored
- Product of his era

## Influence
- Foundation of economics
- Used to justify both regulation and deregulation
- Claimed by all sides
- Moral philosophy often ignored

## Connection to Other Authors
- Friend of Hume (influenced each other)
- Precursor to Hayek (spontaneous order)
- Precursor to Marx (labor theory, but Marx inverted)
- Influenced by Stoics (impartial spectator)
- Contemporary relevance: market vs. planning debates

## The Real Smith
Read both books together — the economist who understood sympathy AND the moralist who understood markets. The full picture is more interesting than the caricature.
